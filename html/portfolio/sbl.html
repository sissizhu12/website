              Slow Burn Leather

              Slow Burn Leather

              introduction

              Slow Burn Leather (SBL) is a Toronto-based small art business that makes leather accessories for fashion and play.

              Although they already catalogued products through an Instagram business account, they wanted to launch an ecommerce website for a more formal customer experience.

              I was brought on as a designer and consultant to personalize their website branding.

              my role
              UI/UX design, UX research

              collaborators
              1 founder, 1 graphic designer

              tools
              Shopify, Autodesk Sketchbook, InsTrack


              the challenge

              Due to time constraints, SBL opted to use a Shopify website template for the base design. I was brought on the project to improve the user experience with my design and coding skills. My two goals were:

              1. Identify design opportunities to better communicate the SBL brand identity
              2. Customize the website with code to build out design choices


              Due to its existing design and code structure, working with a finished website template (shown below) heavily limits new modifications. Thus, before designing, I familiarized myself with Liquid, Shopify’s web templating language, to understand the extent of possible changes that I could make. The learning process included analyzing the code organization beforehand and the language itself on the fly.

              final website design


              how did I get started?
              (user and competitor research)

              I started by analysing the audience of the SBL Instagram account. Using InsTrack, a third party tool, I got the insight that statistically speaking, their followers are most likely female, live in Canada (GTA) or the US (NYC), and English speaking.

              This quantitative data indicated the main demographic of their online followers and potential customers. However, it left unanswered questions about high level behavior and qualitative traits of SBL customers.

              Thus, to more deeply understand the typical SBL customer, I considered the offline events SBL promoted at, assuming that customer real-life behaviors at those events somewhat translate online.

              When observing real-time interactions with customers at those events, I noticed two demographics of shoppers based on the dimension of product knowledge only. Newer customers often browsed more generally without much base understanding of the products and quickly lost interest without enough product information.

              In constrast, more experienced or repeat customers often asked specific, experience-driven questions about products; likely making mental comparisons between current SBL products, their personal collection, and competitors' products. Based on these insights, I created group archetypes to capture these potential behavioral differences (shown on the right) to keep in mind when designing.

              When looking at competitor websites, I noticed that many have bright, playful colours and similar websites due to using the same Shopify templates. They can mainly be differentiated through product photos, typography, and logos, so one insight I realized was to use those elements to distinguish SBL's website from their competitors.

              For instance, the SBL founder and my client wanted jewel tones or muted colours that weren’t too unapproachable. Based on the research, I suggested leaning into cooler or darker jewel tones to be visually distinctive from other brands.

              branding and accessibility


              Since the template layout couldn't be largely modified, my design approach was to use colour as a tool to speak about the brand identity. To keep the brand visually cohesive, I pulled colours from the existing brand logos. This can be seen in the initial sketches, where I played with colour combinations with the same purple and dark red shades from the logos.

              I was also concerned about readability⁠ and visual accessibility, so I ran text and background colour combinations for all the iterative sketches through a colour contrast checker. This ensured the website colour theme met at least the basic Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG AA).

              customisation with code


              Due to working with an existing website template, implementing total design overhauls to cater to the archetypes was unfortunately not an option.

              However, in addition to double checking the quality of product photo and information, some aspects could be improved by visually emphasizing, swapping, or changing specific website elements. In sum, the custom changes I made included:


            • Updating font types, font sizes, and colours to be more readable or accessibility friendly
            • Updating menu layout and incorporating gradient backgrounds
            • Embedding SBL Instagram posts in the website footer
            • Designing and adding graphic elements

              • reflection


              Overall, the website customizations position the brand as bold and vibrant in appearance, but also poetically sensual. By allowing more personality to shine through, the website is more memorable and stands out better against competitors.


              Because the product photos and the Instagram embedded posts feature the founder and face of the brand, these elements also work to humanize SBL and increase approachability and connection with consumers. As such, this project allowed me strategically use research, consumer psychology, design, and code to push a website's effectiveness or purpose further than just the aesthetic.
